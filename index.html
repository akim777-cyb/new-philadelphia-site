Background · Origin

The Founder: Frank McWorter

Born enslaved in South Carolina in 1777, "Free Frank" McWorter mined saltpeter in Kentucky caves and hired out his labor to purchase his wife Lucy's freedom in 1817 and his own in 1819.

He migrated his family to Pike County, Illinois in 1830, bought 80 acres from the federal government in 1835, and in 1836 platted the 144-lot town of New Philadelphia — the first U.S. town legally registered by an African American.

Using lot sales, farming, and his saltpeter operation, Frank bought the freedom of 16 family members over four decades — an outlay equivalent to more than $500,000 today. He died at New Philadelphia in 1854.

Background · Rise

A Town Ahead of Its Time

Integrated from the start

Black and white families lived, worshipped, and ran businesses side by side decades before the Civil War, with a 30 percent Black population at peak and no recorded history of racial violence.

A working frontier economy

The town supported a general store, blacksmith, schoolhouse, church, and stagecoach stop, with tradespeople including farmers, shoemakers, wheelwrights, merchants, cabinet makers, and a preacher.

Underground Railroad station

Frank harbored freedom seekers and his sons guided groups north to Canada, with escapees from Missouri known to swim the Mississippi to reach New Philadelphia.

Background · Decline

Bypassed by the Railroad

In 1869 the Hannibal & Naples Railroad was built two miles north of New Philadelphia. The station went to Barry — and with it, the commerce, the market access, and the future.

Positioning

The Thesis

Pike County is an undervalued market positioned at the intersection of outdoor recreation, American history, and rural affordability. New Philadelphia's new National Park status is the catalyst to unlock it.

Pillar 01

Hunting & Outdoor Economy

Pike County is Illinois' #1 whitetail deer destination, with existing hospitality and outfitter infrastructure.

ABrand the Hunter's Capital

Anchor identity around "Pike County — Illinois' Deer Capital"; outfitter consortium joint marketing; sportsman trade show presence.

BExtend the Season

Waterfowl, turkey, upland bird, fly fishing; agritourism; heritage river tours.

CUpgrade the Hospitality Stack

Mid-scale lodge/B&B development; "Pike County Pass" bundle; heritage Main Street restoration.

$202.3M regional tourism economic impact in 2023 (Adams + Pike + Hancock counties, per Tourism Economics / SeeQuincy).
Pillar 02

Heritage Corridor

2022 NPS designation anchors a Central IL history corridor linking Lincoln's Springfield and Twain's Hannibal.

AThe Free Frontier Trail

Formalize the branded Hannibal → New Philadelphia → Springfield heritage drive.

BOn-site experience upgrade

CInstitutional partnerships

Smithsonian NMAAHC, NMAH, university co-programming, annual academic events.

A growing national heritage-tourism segment with ready-made corridor traffic from Hannibal and Springfield.

Analysis

SWOT

Strengths
  • Federally designated asset: 2022 NPS status confers legitimacy, stewardship funding, and national press.
  • Proven hunting economy: Pike County consistently leads Illinois in deer harvest with mature outfitter/lodge infrastructure.
  • Geographic dividend: bracketed by two major rivers, between Hannibal (Twain) and Springfield (Lincoln).
  • Deep archaeological record: 150,000+ artifacts and partnerships with U. of Illinois, U. of Maryland, and the Smithsonian.
Weaknesses
  • Nothing is above ground — the townsite is an open field, making it hard to "sell" visually to casual travelers.
  • Minimal visitor infrastructure: no on-site visitor center, limited signage, AR tour requires smartphone.
  • Shrinking population base: Pike County −0.6%/yr, Barry −0.81%/yr — weak residential demand and labor market.
  • Remote access: ~1 hr from Peoria airport, 2 hrs from St. Louis — inconvenient for day-trippers.
Opportunities
  • NPS master planning underway — window to shape visitor center siting, trail design, and branding around Barry.
  • Heritage tourism is a growing segment — Adams/Pike/Hancock counties generated $202.3M in 2023 tourism impact.
  • Remote-work migration: affordable land and low density fit the demographic leaving Chicago/St. Louis metros.
  • Adjacent attractions synergy: corridor marketing with Hannibal, Quincy, Springfield captures existing traffic.
Threats
  • Federal funding uncertainty: NPS budget cycles and political appetite for new-park investment fluctuate.
  • Hunting-economy concentration: climate change, chronic wasting disease, or regulation shifts hit the local base.
  • Competing heritage narratives: Hannibal and Springfield are already built out as destinations.
  • Rural broadband and healthcare gaps may cap the remote-worker migration thesis.

The Archive

Nothing stands above ground at New Philadelphia. The townsite is an open field. What remains is the record — the 1836 plat, the artifacts turned up by two decades of archaeological survey, the photograph, the living voice of descendants. This is where the town lives now.

The Original Plat

Material Record

The Artifact Cabinet

A partial inventory of what two decades of excavation, led by Dr. Paul Shackel (University of Maryland) and Dr. Christopher Fennell (University of Illinois), recovered from the site. More than 150,000 items catalogued.

Ceramic sherds

Transfer-print dinnerware fragments. One plate, recovered from the home of Alexander Clark — a Black blacksmith — bears a "Rural Scenery" motif consistent with mid-19th-century English earthenware.

Iron nails & hardware

Square-cut nails, hinge fragments, and door hardware — datable signatures of the town's 1840s–1880s built fabric, recovered from the foundations of identified homes and workshops.

Clay marbles & toys

Hand-shaped clay marbles and miniature figurines — evidence of children at play. Fennell's team flagged these finds as the emotional heart of the dig: the presence of domestic life, not just commerce.

Buttons & personal wares

Bone, shell, and brass buttons; fragments of bone combs; remains of toothbrushes. The quiet signatures of everyday life, recovered in scale across the 14 surveyed lots.

Building foundations

Fourteen intact foundation footprints identified through geophysical survey and targeted excavation. Dr. Fennell: "The foundations are what proved this wasn't just a trash scatter — this was a town."

Gravel street traces

Sub-surface gravel lenses marking the former roadway alignments — including Ann, Broad, Main, and Franklin Streets — still legible through the plow zone after 140 years of agriculture.
